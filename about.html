Origin Story

If you're a millennial of a certain age, you probably have a similar story. Especially if you were one of the "lucky" ones tapped for the Gifted and Talented program—GATE. I don’t know many of us who made it through that program without ending up neurodivergent, nursing a late-in-life mental health diagnosis, or maintaining a high-functioning addiction. Some of us won the lottery and got all three (like me! Too bad that luck doesn’t translate to the Powerball).

I grew up in a small town in southern Arkansas. While I can look back now and mostly remember the good memories, there was plenty of trauma to go around—family dysfunction, being a gay teenager in the "Jesus Freak" and Left Behind era of the South, and watching the post-9/11 world collapse in real-time. It’s a wonder any of us made it out intact. But through all the chaos and bullshit, I had my books. They were my reliable escape from whatever negativity I experienced., where I could be whatever I wanted to be. I was a voracious reader, a massive Star Wars nerd who devoured the EU (now "Legends," I guess—thanks, Disney) novels. I was the kid who practiced his lightsaber fighting techniques in the woods behind the house, and ran a fan site on WebTV. (Look it up if you’re unfamiliar; it was peak early-internet coolness, assuming you don't mind 56k speeds and a keyboard that doubled as a lap-warmer). I spent my childhood lost in worlds that weren’t my own, waiting to see what the future had in store.

My 20s, however, were a blur. I wouldn’t say I necessarily had a unique story—though I did once make a therapist cry while recounting my formative years, so I win therapy—as many have walked similar roads. But from age 20 to 29, I managed to pack in a lifetime of "life changing experiences":

Health & Hardship I developed my first and second major addictions, received my first (and then second, and third) mental health diagnoses to correct the first and second, and declared bankruptcy before age 25.

Professional Cyclone: I dropped out of college for financial reasons and eventually found myself thrust into management roles I was wildly unprepared for (the classic "fake it until you make it" method), forcing me to teach myself how to do a great number of things as I went along (and this was pre-AI days. I did a lot of reading along the way)

Seemingly Endless Grief: I lost my aunt who was my best friend and the one person I could always count on no matter what. I also lost nearly a dozen friends to drug overdoses or suicide due to the general weight of living and shifting social and economic landscapes.

Finding My Anchor: In the middle of the chaos, I got married to the love of my life and moved 900 miles from home. Our relationship was not perfect by a long shot (as our separation in 2020 and subsequent divorce shows), but in those 10 years together I grew so much and I will always be grateful for our time together.

Identity Deconstruction:After the Pulse nightclub shooting, I experienced a total loss of faith, leading to a multi-year deconstruction that saw me walking away from the Church. The one thing I thought I’d always have to lean on for support. For maybe the first time in my adult life, I felt truly alone. The first, but not the last, time I experienced it, and I hated it.

I spent most of those years either in a deep depression or self-medicating. Turns out, undiagnosed mental illness and a fundamentally broken system will do that to you. As my 20s came to a close and I prepared to enter "gay death", I was at the height of my self esteem, never felt better about who I was, what I had to offer, I was in school, and the future was so damn bright. How could I have known what was waiting for us around the corner? Divorce, moving back home in the middle of a global pandemic, starting over from nothing...again...My early 30s were a testament to perseverance and the strength of my community.

Now, in my mid-30s, I’ve experienced my final "rock bottom" (hopefully the last one, anyway). For the first time, I truly lost everything. So, from the ashes of the past, I’m finally building the future I should have started a decade ago. This site, this mentality, this (hopefully) lifestyle of "Bearly in a hurry" is part trying to find/build community, part public accountability, and part "fuck it, better late than never." I’m learning in public, building community around ideas that matter, and trying to create the world I was promised instead of just accepting the one I inherited—even if, for now, that world only exists in my own mind.

Who I'm Becoming

So, what did the past 30 something odd years lead to? What kind of person emerged from the crucible of all those experiences? The jury is still out on that one. But here is what I can say about life right now and what it could be:

Shedding Masks: I said to my therapist at the beginning of 2025 that I was worried there wasn't a "me" left. That I had spent so many years being so many things to so many people that I no longer had a distinct identity or personality. That I only exist in relation to whoever I am with at any given moment. The one good thing I can say about my last relationship is that he actually helped me begin this process. No matter the reality of our relationship, when I was with him I can honestly say I was 100% comfortable and felt free to be myself. He would listen to my long-winded speeches on topics that I found interesting ( even though he usually couldn't care any less about the content), I never felt like I had to filter myself, and it was in that environment that I began to wonder "is this how people feel all the time?" It has been quite odd and very challenging to confront this idea and start to live in a way that is authentic to whoever that person is. It's a process filled with trial and error (a lot of error), but it's necessary if I am ever going to be truly happy and fulfilled in this life.

Building Things: My motivations for action have shifted and I no longer am driven by the desire for money, status, or authority. I used to think those things were what I should be working for, the only things that would make me happy. However I have found that this mentality has led to people monetizing their hobbies, embracing hustle culture, and missing out on their own lives. It's not their fault, its what culture became. So I am working on building things that may go absolutely nowhere. As I type these words I am aware that I may be the only person who ever reads these words, but they exist. I existed, my work existed, and I created it just for the experience of doing so. Here are some examples of things I am building that could lead to something, or could be just me throwing sand into the wind:

* Tutoring Business: I started an LLC last year and it has taken many forms in that time. During my long unemployment, I rediscovered my love of tutoring and helping students find their own way of doing things. At first I approached it as a hustle, and it was a nightmare. I tutored every subject I could get approved for through tutoring websites and spending a lot of time working with students in subject that I hated. So I transitioned to my own entity called Longhand Compass where I focus on the subjects, skills, and ages I do best with. Through this process I have learned a lot about curriculum development, teaching methodologies, expanded my knowledge in cognitive psychology pertaining to memory and learning, developed a lesson plan database and helped students realize they are capable of so much more than they think. While working for tutoring websites I had great experiences, like helping a first generation college hopeful craft her personal statement for her UC application. It was a truly humbling experience. Or Spending a school year with a boy who couldn't string together more than a few words at the start to him eventually creating a long form narrative involving a legendary sword and the knight who was destined to wield it was a grueling but rewarding experience. Since branching out solo I have had exactly one student and you know, I am okay with it. I learned how web hosting works, I coded my own website using AI to work the code, so while I hope that I can get better at marketing and such and have an income, I am still learning along the way, and that is valuabel in its own right

* Digital Garden/PKM System/Personal Wiki: I have been a slave to hierarchical, nested folder storage systems my entire life. My notebooks and now computers have always had a very meticulous taxonomy that was constantly in flux. The aesthetics had to be just right, and I had to make sure that everything was tagged and nested correctly so I could find it later. Then I came across Obsidian and began reading through peoples mind gardens and I fell in love with the ideas I was reading. People, adults, interested in knowledge work, learning for the sake of learning, discussing ideas and letting those ideas flow and connect in novel ways. Which led me to dive into productivity systems like Get Things Done (GTD), note taking strategies like Zettelkasten, and being introduced to the IndieWeb and the cool ideas that can be found over there. This may all fall apart tomorrow. Or it may help me build a system that finally helps me work with knowledge and information in a way that I have always wanted. A system that allows me to help others understand me better because everything is on the table and you are there every step of the way (almost)

Learning Everything: I was the kid who hated summer break. Being away from school for that long was my own personal hell. Now, it's 2025 and we can learn so much on Beyonce's internet. I am taking classes online in different areas, reading topical information on subjects I find interesting, I have a folder with about 30 journal articles from various scientific journals that I am working through and writing lit reviews on. Just because I can. Obsidian is helping with this a lot as is Khan Academy, Coursera/EdX, and just plain old searching. At the time of this writing, my current research list reads as: Personal README/User Manuals, How to work with knowledge as a product, Neighborhood Association Structure and Procedures, Grant writing procedures. I am also taking certificate classes in LLM prompt engineering, Intro to Negotiation Strategy, Omens Oracles and Prophecy, How to Write Effective Policies and Procedures Manuals, and Understanding Learning Styles. We have access to so much information, a lot of it free access through your local library or universities, you just need to look. These classes and such will probably lead to nothing, be taken seriously by nobody, but I gained new knowledge and skilled, and that is worth it.

Showing Up: Last year a motivating factor for quitting my job was to become more civically involved. It took me a while, but I can say that I am finally doing just that. I have learned a lot over the past year about how these organizations and movements actually work and how the city government operates and how I as a citizen fits in with the process. After completing the R.O.C.K. Academy I fostered connections with city leaders which has led to me working on restarting the neighborhood association for my neighborhood, I watch the LR Board of Directors meetings most weeks, I even am applying to try and get a position on one of the city commission positions that are open. It is a pretty exciting time and I didn't realize until recently how much there is for citizens to do. I have been doing a lot of work with Get Loud! Arkansas, a voting rights group that helps people register to vote, understand their rights as voters, etc. I have been doing text banking and attending different marches and gatherings to set up camp and see if anyone needs help getting registered. Lots of pokers in the fire, but it feels good to be out there doing work. I have wanted to get more involved in working for a better Little Rock. I love this city and we have so much potential. There are people who want to do the work, we just need to get then organized.

At the end of the day I am basically an underdeveloped multipotentialite , work-in-progress, and survivor of the "scenic route." I will never be what 10-year old me dreamed about at this stage of my life. I used to feel some sort of grief about that, but by living a life that is more aligned with my values, daring to believe in a world that is built on equity and compassion, maybe I can achieve something better for that little guy I still carry in my heart as my guiding light. If you read this and think "wow, that sounds familiar", I say, welcome to the garden. Let's learn together.
