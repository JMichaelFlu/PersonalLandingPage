How I Got Here

I learned early in life that things were not going to be easy, and I was not given the most winning of hands. In a chaotic environment filled with great intentions yet subpar results, I got good at predicting emotions and bad at having needs. I could tell you what everyone in the room was feeling and usually diffuse tense situations, but never learned how to handle my own feelings or ask for help.

So I did what kids do and immersed myself in the worlds I found in books. I didn't understand what I was doing at the time; I just knew that when I existed in those worlds I felt excited, alive, and free from expectations. I didn't have words for it. I simply had my stories.

I grew up in a small town in southern Arkansas. I can look back now and mostly remember the good memories, but there was plenty of trauma to go around—family dysfunction, watching the post-9/11 world collapse in real time, and learning early how to stay quiet, stay useful, and not take up too much space. Life wasn't always easy, I often felt alone, but through all the chaos and bullshit, I had my books.

I was a voracious reader and a massive Star Wars nerd who devoured the EU novels with great speed. I practiced lightsaber techniques in the woods behind the house and ran a fan site on WebTV. (Look up WebTV if you're unfamiliar; it was peak early-internet coolness, assuming you don't mind 56k speeds and loved your Tab button.)

My 20s were a lot. Trying to tell that decade straight through would turn this page into a book, so here are the highlights—what happened, and what it did to me.

  • Health & Hardship: I developed my first and second major addictions, received my first (and then second, and third) mental health diagnoses to correct the first and second, and declared bankruptcy before age 25.
  • College (and the Consequences): I left college halfway through my sophomore fall. Freshman year was too much fun and not enough focus, and the jump from coasting in high school to a private D3 (Division III) school's academic rigor put me on academic probation. That dominoed into reduced aid; when my parents chose not to co-sign renewed private loans, I couldn't afford to stay—even though I still think I could've turned it around if I'd had the runway.
  • Work and Worth: Despite my lack of formal training and on-the-job support, I was able to secure—and excel in—management roles early in my career. I carried an immense amount of shame and disappointment about not finishing college and being "just a server," and management offered me a chance to earn the respect and legitimacy I craved during that phase of my life—so I learned on the fly, built the plane in the air, and taught myself what the job didn't, just to keep the wheels on.
  • Grief: I will never forget how it felt at age 14 to hear that a classmate and friend had taken his own life. The memory is still vivid, and I'd like to say it gets easier over time—but that would be a lie. Thankfully, I didn't experience that again for many years. But (if you'll excuse the dark humor) as Smash Mouth told us: when the days start coming, they don't stop comin'.
  • More Grief: In 2012, my best friend—basically my other half—died from an accidental overdose related to complications from multiple sclerosis (MS). A year or two later, her partner—also my close friend—was gone. Nothing was ever definitively found, to my knowledge, but the circumstances of her crushing depression after the loss were clearly part of the story.
  • The Big One: In 2015, my aunt Mary—my dad's sister, my best friend, and my fiercest advocate, protector, and confidant—died suddenly from a pulmonary embolism (PE). She was the first person I came out to in my family. She made me feel loved and safe, unconditionally. Her death didn't just devastate our family; it altered the course of my life. I try hard not to live in a mindset of regret, but I will never forgive myself for the last time we talked: after a Razorback tailgate—drunk on most of a bottle of Jack Daniel's and blacked out with no memory of the event—I spoke the last words I would ever speak to her, heard her voice for the last time, and I don't even remember it. I will never get that memory back, and it haunts me even now.
  • The Ongoing Part: The list unfortunately continues and takes far too much space to share here. In the ten years from 2015 to now (2025), I have lost no fewer than ten friends to addiction or suicide. I carry each one in my heart and try to do something in their memory every year. If you're struggling with depression or suicidal thoughts, you can call or text 988 (U.S.) and find support through the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention.
  • Finding My Anchor: In the middle of the chaos, I got married to the love of my life and moved 900 miles from home. Our relationship wasn't perfect (as our separation in 2020 and subsequent divorce shows), but in those 10 years together I grew so much, and I will always be grateful for our time together.
  • Identity Deconstruction: After the Pulse nightclub shooting, I experienced a total loss of faith, leading to a multi-year deconstruction that saw me walking away from the Church—the one thing I thought I'd always have to lean on for support. For maybe the first time in my adult life, I felt truly alone. The first, but not the last, time I experienced it, and I hated it.

I spent most of those years either in a deep depression or self-medicating—usually both. It was easier than dealing with what I was carrying around in my head and heart, at least in the short term. When I was drinking, the world felt simpler. I felt liked. I didn't hurt as much. I could laugh more easily. And no matter how many problems it caused, no matter how much pain I inflicted while in its grasp, I treated the bottle like a friend and a lifeline for almost two decades—until I finally learned how to walk without it wrapped around my neck.

I finally felt like the person I was supposed to be. My confidence was at its peak, I was back in school following my passions, and for the first time in a long time, I was hopeful for the future. I forgot how quickly things could change in this unpredictable world. I didn't see what was waiting around the corner—and I wasn't as prepared as I thought I was.

I'd already started over once—moving to Wisconsin with no friends, no job, and no family nearby, leaving my Arkansas life behind and building from scratch. Then I did it again when I came back home: no job, few prospects, a pandemic shrinking what support existed, and most of my family (except two siblings) already gone. My early 30s were a testament to perseverance and the strength of my community.

Now, in my mid-30s, I hit a rock bottom I don't want to revisit. For the first time, I felt like I'd truly lost everything. Not literally—but the scaffolding was gone: I was unemployed, flirting with repossession every day, and I'd alienated almost all of my friends. I didn't have much of a reason to wake up, and that made it brutally hard to build any motivation for life.

So, I'm finally building the future I should have started a decade ago. This site, this mentality, this (hopefully) lifestyle of "Bearly in a hurry" is part trying to find/build community, part public accountability, and part "fuck it, better late than never." I'm learning in public, building community around ideas that matter, and trying to create the world I was promised instead of just accepting the one I inherited—even if, for now, that world only exists in my own mind.

Who I'm Becoming

So what did the past 30-something years lead to? What kind of person emerged from the crucible of all those experiences? The jury is still out. But here is what I can say about life right now and what it could be.

Shedding Masks: I said to my therapist at the beginning of 2025 that I was worried there wasn't a "me" left—that I had spent so many years being so many things to so many people that I no longer had a distinct identity or personality, that I only exist in relation to whoever I am with at any given moment. The one good thing I can say about my last relationship is that he actually helped me begin this process. No matter the reality of our relationship, when I was with him I can honestly say I was 100% comfortable and felt free to be myself. He would listen to my long-winded speeches on topics that I found interesting (even though he usually couldn't care less about the content), I never felt like I had to filter myself, and it was in that environment that I began to wonder, "Is this how people feel all the time?" It has been odd and challenging to confront this idea and start to live in a way that is authentic to whoever that person is. It's a process filled with trial and error (a lot of error), but it's necessary if I am ever going to be truly happy and fulfilled in this life.

Building Things: My motivations for action have shifted, and I'm no longer driven by the desire for money, status, or authority. I used to think those things were what I should be working for—the only things that would make me happy. In practice, that mentality pushes people to monetize their hobbies, embrace hustle culture, and miss out on their own lives. It's not their fault; it's what culture became. I'm working on building things that may never find a big audience, and I am fine with that. I hope people find something helpful in what I make—something relatable, or a little hope for their own stories—but my motivation doesn't depend on it. I want to honor what's inside my heart and create, not chase a scoreboard. This might be a message in a bottle—maybe it reaches a handful of people, maybe it washes back up on my own shore. Either way, I'm still sending it.

Here are some examples of things I am building that could lead to something, or could be just me throwing sand into the wind:

  • Tutoring Business: I started an LLC last year and it has taken many forms in that time. During my long unemployment, I rediscovered my love of tutoring and helping students find their own way of doing things. At first I approached it as a hustle, and it was a nightmare: I tutored every subject I could get approved for through tutoring websites and spent a lot of time working with students in subjects that I hated. So I transitioned to my own entity called Longhand Compass where I focus on the subjects, skills, and ages I do best with. Through this process I've learned a lot about curriculum development, teaching methodologies, and cognitive psychology as it pertains to memory and learning; I've developed a lesson plan database; and I've helped students realize they are capable of so much more than they think. While working for tutoring websites I had great experiences—like helping a first-generation college hopeful craft her personal statement for her UC application, or spending a school year with a boy who couldn't string together more than a few words at the start and eventually wrote a long-form narrative involving a legendary sword and the knight destined to wield it. Since branching out solo, I've had exactly one student. And you know what? I'm okay with it. I learned how web hosting works. I coded my own website using AI to work the code. So while I hope I can get better at marketing and earn a stable income, I'm still learning along the way, and that's valuable in its own right.
  • Digital Garden / PKM System / Personal Wiki: I have been a slave to hierarchical, nested folder storage systems my entire life. My notebooks (and now computers) have always had a meticulous taxonomy that was constantly in flux. The aesthetics had to be just right, and everything had to be tagged and nested correctly so I could find it later. Then I came across Obsidian and began reading through people's mind gardens, and I fell in love with the ideas I was finding: adults interested in knowledge work, learning for the sake of learning, discussing ideas, and letting those ideas connect in novel ways. That led me to productivity systems like Get Things Done (GTD), note-taking strategies like Zettelkasten, and the IndieWeb. This may all fall apart tomorrow. Or it may help me build a system that finally lets me work with knowledge and information the way I've always wanted—a system that allows others to understand me better because everything is on the table and you're there for the process (almost).

Learning Everything: I was the kid who hated summer break. Being away from school for that long was my own personal hell. Now it's 2025 and we can learn so much on Beyoncé's internet. I am taking classes online in different areas, reading topical information on subjects I find interesting, and I have a folder with about 30 journal articles from various scientific journals that I am working through and writing lit reviews on—just because I can. Obsidian is helping with this a lot, as is Khan Academy, Coursera/EdX, and plain old searching. At the time of this writing, my current research list reads as: Personal README/User Manuals, how to work with knowledge as a product, neighborhood association structure and procedures, and grant-writing procedures. I am also taking certificate classes in LLM prompt engineering, Intro to Negotiation Strategy, Omens Oracles and Prophecy, How to Write Effective Policies and Procedures Manuals, and Understanding Learning Styles. We have access to so much information—much of it free through your local library or universities—you just need to look. Even if these classes don't lead to new opportunities or advancement, I'm learning again, and I am loving the process.

Showing Up: Last year a motivating factor for quitting my job was to become more civically involved. It took me a while, but I can say that I am finally doing just that. I've learned a lot over the past year about how these organizations and movements actually work, how city government operates, and how I as a citizen fit in the process. After completing the R.O.C.K. Academy I fostered connections with city leaders, which has led to me working on restarting the neighborhood association for my neighborhood. I watch the LR Board of Directors meetings most weeks. I'm even applying to try and get a position on one of the city commission roles that are open. It is a pretty exciting time, and I didn't realize until recently how much there is for citizens to do. I've also been doing work with Get Loud! Arkansas, a voting rights group that helps people register to vote and understand their rights as voters. I've been text-banking and attending different marches and gatherings to set up camp and see if anyone needs help getting registered. Lots of pokers in the fire, but it feels good to be out there doing work. I love this city and we have so much potential. There are people who want to do the work; we just need to get them organized.

At the end of the day I am basically an underdeveloped multipotentialite, a work-in-progress, and a survivor of the scenic route. I won't be what 10-year-old me imagined. But I'm trying to become someone he'd be proud of—someone he's glad he turned into. If you read this and think "wow, that sounds familiar," I say: welcome to the garden. Let's learn together.
