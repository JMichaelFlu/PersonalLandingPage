How I Got Here

As a kid, I became really good at reading the room. I could predict peoples moods and became adept at diffusing tension, making sure everyone around me was happy. It's what you do when your daily life is filled with a lot of conflict and chaos. I was one of those "so mature for your age" kids. Adults let me hang out in the room while the other kids were sent away for being "too much". I wanted to be helpful, liked, useful, so I did everything I could to be all those things. But more than that, I also wanted to be invisible. Attention was risky. Being seen meant being vulnerable. What I never learned was how to have my own needs, or handle my own emotions. I could tell you what everyone in the room was feeling and how to best navigate around those feelings, but never how to deal with them when they were my own. So I did what a lot of kids do when the real world feels like a minefield: I escaped into books and fantasy. In those worlds, I didn't want to be invisible, I could finally live.

I grew up in a small town in southern Arkansas, with a little bit of time on the coast of North Carolina (divorced parents offered some options). I was a voracious reader and a massive Star Wars nerd who tore through all the EU novels, practiced lightsaber fighting in the woods behind my house, and ran a fan site on WebTV. (Look it up if you're unfamiliar; it was peak early-internet coolness, assuming you loved your Tab button.) Obviously I was very cool. Truth is, I kind of was in a way. Being a chameleon meant I was able to be whoever I needed to be for any given situation. Every group and person got their own curated version of me, designed to be the perfect fit for them. It has led to a lifetime of me wondering who I really am at the end of the day.

All of this led me to becoming a neurodivergent millennial with a late diagnosed mental health condition who took the scenic route through life. It hasn't always been pretty, but it's been filled with lessons that will hopefully inform the second half of my life.

The Road Ahead

So what kind of person emerged from all those experiences? The jury is still out. But here is what I can say about life right now and what I hope for the future:

Shedding Masks: I said to my therapist at the beginning of 2025 that I was worried there wasn't a "me" left—that I had spent so many years being so many things to so many people that I no longer had a distinct identity or personality, that I only exist in relation to whoever I am with at any given moment. The one good thing I can say about my last relationship is that he actually helped me begin this process. No matter the reality of our relationship, when I was with him I can honestly say I was 100% comfortable and felt free to be myself. He would listen to my long-winded speeches on topics that I found interesting (even though he usually couldn't care less about the content), I never felt like I had to filter myself, and it was in that environment that I began to wonder, "Is this how people feel all the time?" It has been odd and challenging to confront this idea and start to live in a way that is authentic to whoever that person is. It's a process filled with trial and error (a lot of error), but it's necessary if I am ever going to be truly happy and fulfilled in this life.

Building Things: My motivations for action have shifted, and I'm no longer driven by the desire for money, status, or authority. I used to think those things were what I should be working for—the only things that would make me happy. In practice, that mentality pushes people to monetize their hobbies, embrace hustle culture, and miss out on their own lives. It's not their fault; it's what culture became. I'm working on building things that may never find a big audience, and I am fine with that. I hope people find something helpful in what I make. They find something relatable, or a little hope for their own stories, but my motivation to write and create doesn't depend on it. All of this might be a message in a bottle tossed into the ocean; maybe it reaches someone, maybe it washes back up on my own shore. Either way, I'm still sending it. Here are some examples of things I am building:

  • Tutoring Business: I started an LLC last year and it has taken many forms in that time. During my long unemployment, I rediscovered my love of helping others learn things. At first I approached it as a hustle, and it was a nightmare: I tutored every subject I could get approved for through tutoring websites and spent a lot of time working with students in subjects that I hated. So I transitioned to my own entity called Longhand Compass where I focus on the subjects, skills, and ages I do best with. Through this process I've learned a lot about curriculum development, teaching methodologies, and cognitive psychology as it pertains to memory and learning; I've developed a lesson plan database; and I've helped students realize they are capable of so much more than they think. While working for tutoring websites I had great experiences—like helping a first-generation college hopeful craft her personal statement for her UC application, or spending a school year with a boy who couldn't string together more than a few words at the start and eventually wrote a long-form narrative involving a legendary sword and the knight destined to wield it. Since branching out solo, I've had exactly one student. And you know what? I'm okay with it. I learned how web hosting works. I coded my own website using AI to work the code. So while I hope I can get better at marketing and earn a stable income, I'm still learning along the way, and that's valuable in its own right.
  • Digital Garden: I have been a slave to hierarchical, nested folder storage systems my entire life. My notebooks (and now computers) have always had a meticulous taxonomy that was constantly in flux. The aesthetics had to be just right, and everything had to be tagged and nested correctly so I could find it later. Then I came across Obsidian and began reading through people's mind gardens, and I fell in love with the ideas I was finding: adults interested in knowledge work, learning for the sake of learning, discussing ideas, and letting those ideas connect in novel ways. That led me to productivity systems like Get Things Done (GTD), note-taking strategies like Zettelkasten, and the IndieWeb. This may all fall apart tomorrow. Or it may help me build a system that finally lets me work with knowledge and information the way I've always wanted; a system that allows others to understand me better because everything is on the table and you're there for the process (almost).

Learning Everything: I was the kid who hated summer break. Being away from school for that long was my own personal hell. Now it's 2025 and we can learn anything on Beyoncé's internet, so why not do it? I am taking classes online in different areas, reading topical information on subjects I find interesting, and I have a folder with about 30 journal articles from various scientific journals that I am working through and writing lit reviews on, just because I can. Obsidian is helping with this a lot, as is Khan Academy, Coursera/EdX, and plain old searching. At the time of this writing, my current research list reads as: Personal README/User Manuals, how to work with knowledge as a product, neighborhood association structure and procedures, and grant-writing procedures. I am also taking certificate classes in LLM prompt engineering, Negotiation Strategy, Omens Oracles and Prophecy, How to Write Effective Policies and Procedures Manuals, and Understanding Learning Styles. We have access to so much information (much of it free through your local library or universities) you just need to look. Even if these classes don't lead to new opportunities or advancement, I'm learning again, and I am loving the process.

Showing Up: Last year a motivating factor for quitting my job was to become more civically involved. It took me a while, but I can say that I am finally doing just that. I've learned a lot over the past year about how these organizations and movements actually work, how city government operates, and how I as a citizen fit in the process. After completing the R.O.C.K. Academy I fostered connections with city leaders, which has led to me working on restarting the neighborhood association for my neighborhood. I watch the LR Board of Directors meetings most weeks. I'm even applying to try and get a position on one of the city commission roles that are open. It is a pretty exciting time, and I didn't realize until recently how much there is for citizens to do. I've also been doing work with Get Loud! Arkansas, a voting rights group that helps people register to vote and understand their rights as voters. I've been text-banking and attending different marches and gatherings to set up camp and see if anyone needs help getting registered. Lots of pokers in the fire, but it feels good to be out there doing work. I love this city and we have so much potential.

At the end of the day I am basically an underdeveloped multipotentialite, a work-in-progress, and a survivor of the scenic route. I won't be what 10-year-old me imagined. But I'm trying to become someone he'd be proud of—someone he's glad he turned into.
