Origin Story

If you're a millennial of a certain age, you probably have a similar story. Especially if you were one of the "lucky" ones tapped for the gifted and talented program - GATE. I don't know many who were in that program who didn't turn out neurodivergent, with a late mental health diagnosis, or a high-functioning addiction. Some of us won the lottery and got all three (like me! Too bad that luck doesn't translate to the real lottery).

I grew up in a small town in southern Arkansas. While I can look back now and find the good memories, there was plenty of trauma - family dysfunction, being a gay teenager in the "Jesus Freak" and Left Behind era in the south, and the post-9/11 world collapsing around all of us. It's a wonder any of us made it out intact. But I had my books. I was a voracious reader, a massive Star Wars nerd who read all the EU (now Legends I guess) novels, knew every piece of trivia, and even ran a fan site on WebTV (look it up if you're unfamiliar, it was peak early internet coolness). That's how I spent my childhood: lost in worlds that weren't my own, waiting to see what the future had in store.

My 20s were a blur. In that decade of my life I was able to pack in so much transformative change. I wouldn't say that I necessarily had a unique story, (but I did make a therapist cry once with talking about my growing up years, so draw your own conclusions) as many have walked similar roads, but from age 20-29 I:

  • Developed my first and second major addictions
  • Declared bankruptcy
  • Dropped out of college for financial reasons
  • Thrust into a professional role I was not prepared for
  • Received my first mental health diagnosis
  • Received my second (and third at the same time) to correct the first one
  • Lost my aunt, who was my best friend and the one person I knew I could always count on in this world
  • Lost my best friend to a drug overdose
  • Lost another friend to drug overdose
  • Lost no less than 5 more friends during that time, almost all self medicating against the realities and trauma of life
  • Got married to the love of my life and moved 900 miles from home
  • Experienced a loss of faith after the events of the Pulse nightclub shooting, which led to a multi year deconstruction process from the one thing in my life that I thought I would always have to lean on

I spent most of those years either in a deep depression or self medicating. Turns out, undiagnosed mental illness and a fundamentally broken system will do that to you. Add it all together and you have the blueprint for "the scenic route."

Now, in my mid-30s, I have experienced my final "rock bottom" and for the first time truly lost everything. So from the ashes of the past I'm finally building the future I should have built a decade ago. This site is part digital garden, part public accountability, part "fuck it, better late than never." I'm learning in public, building community around ideas that matter, and trying to create the world I was promised instead of accepting the one I inherited, even if it's only in my own mind

What I'm About

I had originally planned to attend the Ohio State University for college but Hendrix College found its way into my heart and wouldn't let go. I was the perfect candidate for admission, and I bought into the mission and the dream that my classmates and I were going to change the world and be these amazing researchers and public servants and leaders. Many of my classmates became just that, and then theres Maude (me). I'm still finding my way, and despite it all, have somehow maintained that naive optimistic heart that has carried me through.

In a different life, I wanted to teach. However, academia became more and more of a pipe dream until I knew that it was time to put that dream on the shelf and focus my energies on something more attainable. But the fact remains that I love to help and teach others, so I run a tutoring business called Longhand Compass, working with students and parents who are navigating the same overburdened education system I came up through, offering them the individualized attention and plans they need to succeed. I care about making learning accessible, human, and useful instead of performative.

I believe in the power of community and collaboration in all of their many forms. I have longed to create a space for community and formative action. As a cis-gender, white, male, I need to be amplifying voices that matter, doing the work to be anti-racist and a better ally (through self educating, giving of my time and talents, and staying out of the way) and helping shape the future of my city for all of its residents. Change begins in our own back yard and I have been engaging with local service and civic organizations to put my money where my mouth is.
